The shop: the broker in the middle

Why every road goes through one Odoo-run broker — and how to talk to it without ever seeing a Telnyx or Wazo key.

⏱ ~11 minutes🎯 Goal: know which side of the counter any given change belongs on🧩 Builds on: Lessons 1, 6

Picture a hotel with one concierge desk. Guests never get the master keys — not to the phone room, not to the switchboard closet. You hand over a request slip ("please wire a number to desk 1001") and the concierge walks the building for you. Two things fall out of that arrangement: you can't lose keys you never had, and the hotel can renovate the back corridors without reprinting a single slip. The Parrot "shop" — the IAP phone_service — is that concierge. Your customer database only ever writes slips.

Every slip your DB writes lands on one of two route families. Business routes (/api/phone_service/1/<name>) are about numbers and paperwork: search, order, requirements, coverage — the Lesson-6 story. PBX ops (/api/phone_service/1/pbx/<op>) are about your floor in the switchboard: sync a user's extension, route an incall, set up a voicemail. Both go through the same client, PhoneServiceAPI, which stamps the same four credentials onto every call: client_id, client_secret, account_token (your IAP credits account) and db_uuid.

Getting a counter account — no signup form

Nobody types a Telnyx key into Odoo settings, and nobody types a shop password either. The first time your DB talks to the shop, PhoneServiceAPI auto-registers (register_client): it generates a random client_id and client_secret locally and presents them. The shop, crucially, never stores your secret — only an HMAC hash of it (client_secret_hash), checked with a constant-time compare on every request. If a call ever comes back authentication_failed, the client re-registers once and retries — self-healing, no human involved.onboarding §14

That "hash only" choice has a neat consequence. The shop also sends you webhooks (the doorbell you met in Lesson 5 — Lesson 9 goes deep), and it must sign them — but with what? It can't sign with your secret; it only kept the fingerprint. So at registration it derives a purpose-bound signing key — HMAC(client_secret, "iap-phone-service-webhook-signing") — and stores that. Both sides can compute it; nobody else can. The old signing key was client_id… which travels in the body of every request. A key that rides on the outside of the envelope is not a key.

The big idea: ask for outcomes, not for objects

The PBX API is capability-style. Your DB asks the shop for an outcome — sync_user_extension, sync_incall, sync_voicemail — and the shop's private _op_* methods perform the Wazo choreography: which confd objects to create, in what order, under what names. The customer side never learns any of it.

Back at the hotel: you don't ask the concierge for "the boiler-room key so I can open valve 3, then flip breaker 7". You ask for "make room 12 warm". Which valves, in which order — the concierge's private business. Contrast this with a resource passthrough API (/wazo/users, /wazo/lines, /wazo/extensions…): every customer DB would need to know Wazo's object model, its creation order, its naming rules — and the day Odoo wanted to change any of that (or swap out Wazo entirely), it would have to upgrade every customer database on Earth in lockstep. With outcome-shaped slips, the shop can renovate freely: even SIP usernames are invented server-side — enterprise never constructs a Wazo name.onboarding §2

Error keys are the contract

When a slip fails, the shop answers with a stable machine key plus structured details: {"error": "invalid_address", "details": {"invalid_address_fields": ["postal_code"]}}. On the enterprise side, _phone_service_error_message maps ~18 known keys to translated user messages (unknown key → generic "temporarily unavailable"), and PhoneServiceError.error_key is what code branches on — e.g. not_found ⇒ drop the stale PBX ids and re-sync (heal). The prose in error_msg is free to change any day; the keys are frozen.

If you ever match on an error message string, you've built a bug. Messages get reworded, translated, and improved without notice — that's the whole point of keeping them out of the contract. Raw server text is never shown to a user and never parsed by code. Branch on error_key, render locally, always.

Whose number is it anyway? Ownership & the two ledgers

The shop serves many customer DBs out of one Telnyx account — so what stops client A from releasing client B's number? Every Telnyx resource the shop creates is stamped customer_reference = client_id, and that stamp is the sole ownership marker. The doctrine comment atop phone_service_number.py puts it plainly:

"That reference is the ONLY thing that marks a Telnyx number as ours: every Telnyx lookup on the release/reconcile paths filters on it server-side AND re-checks it locally."phone_service_number.py:22–109

Controllers enforce the same rule at the counter: the _assert_owns / _create_owned helpers check every incoming resource id against phone_service.client_external_resource before touching it — a slip naming someone else's resource is refused, not forwarded. That's the anti-IDOR line: you can't even ask about what isn't yours.

And a rule you'll lean on in Lesson 10: a number lives in two ledgers at once — voip.did.number in your DB, phone_service.number in the shop. Neither is authoritative alone. Webhooks push changes to you, hourly polls pull what webhooks missed, and a daily reconcile tears down drift (never re-provisions — creating is the ordering saga's job).

🔌 Where this lives in our code
  • Enterprise — voip/models/phone_service_api.py: _call_phone_service() (auth stamping, one re-register retry), pbx_call(name, **params), PhoneServiceError.error_key, _phone_service_error_message. voip/models/pbx_service.py: the named facade — one method per PBX op, each a one-line pbx_call.
  • Shop — controllers/common.py: @require_client, the require_str/int/… validators, _assert_owns/_create_owned. Routes split into client / number / pbx / regulatory / webhook_controller.py. utils/errors.py: the 17 typed exceptions that serialize to stable keys. models/phone_service_pbx_tenant_ops.py: the private _op_* choreography.
