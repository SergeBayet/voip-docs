Outbound: calling the world

Out through the trunk door — with your own number stamped on the envelope, by the switchboard, not by you.

⏱ ~10 minutes🎯 Goal: trace an outbound call + explain who stamps caller ID now (and why it moved)🧩 Builds on: Lessons 2–3
Start simple 🧒

You want to call a normal phone out in the world — say a customer's mobile. Think of it like mailing a letter:

  • You drop the letter in your company's mailroom (that's Wazo, our switchboard — Lesson 1's building).
  • The mailroom carries it to the post office (Telnyx, our carrier — it has the trucks that reach every phone in the world).
  • Every envelope needs a return address. On a phone call, that's the caller ID — the "who's calling" your customer sees on their screen.
  • The post office only mails your letter if the return address is really yours. Fake one, and it hands the letter back.

The twist of this lesson: who writes the return address? It used to be you, with your own pen (and that went wrong). Today the mailroom stamps it for you, from its own records.

The journey, as a picture

The players are exactly Lesson 1's. Your browser is registered to one place only: the Wazo switchboard, via the single "Odoo Phone Service" provider (wss://wazo-1.voip.test.odoo.com/ws). Kamailio (the signaling edge) forwards your INVITE to Asterisk (the engine), which matches it against the tenant's outcall patterns — _X. and _+X., i.e. "any digits, with or without a leading +" — and sends it out the trunk, the permanent thick wire to Telnyx, which bridges onto the worldwide phone network (the PSTN).(onboarding §7 & §10)

Two side notes, each one sentence. Colleagues: if you dial a colleague's public DID, the softphone resolves it to their internal extension before sending the INVITE — otherwise the call would loop out the trunk and back in, losing your identity on the way. Odoo's call card: the voip.call row is created by the browser (create_and_format) and later updated by PBX events flowing through the shop — that pipeline is Lessons 5 and 9.

The return-address drama (a history lesson that still pays rent)

In the first Parrot prototype there was no switchboard in the path: the browser registered directly to sip.telnyx.com. And outbound calls died instantly. Why? By default the SIP library writes your login name into the From header — the return-address slot — because that's all it knows. Telnyx looked at it and refused: error D35, "Caller Origination Number is Invalid".

The old fix exploited a real SIP feature: an INVITE can carry several caller-ID headers, and Telnyx trusts some more than others:RFC 3325

PPID — P-Preferred-Identityhighest—
PAID — P-Asserted-Identityhigh ← the old hackbrowser JavaScript
RPID — Remote-Party-Idmedium—
Fromlowest (the default)SIP.js, automatically

So the browser added a P-Asserted-Identity header carrying a real DID, out-ranking the junk in From. It worked — and the whole envelope story, era by era:

❌ Old world (D35)
INVITE → Telnyx   From: u42-clienta
Telnyx: "that's not a number I gave you." → rejected (D35)
🩹 The browser hack
INVITE → Telnyx   P-Asserted-Identity: +18076958558 ← written by browser JS
Accepted — but the browser is asserting its own identity…
✅ Today
INVITE → Wazo → fresh trunk leg → Telnyx   caller ID: +18076958558 ← stamped by the switchboard from server config
Accepted — and the browser had no say in it.
⚠️ Why the old way was dangerous. That PAID header was written in browser JavaScript — which any determined user can edit. A user could assert someone else's DID and spoof their caller ID, made worse by everyone sharing one Telnyx connection. Rule to keep forever: the browser is untrusted; identity must be asserted by a server. That spoofing class is dead today: your INVITE terminates at Wazo, and the leg that reaches Telnyx is a fresh call whose identity comes from the switchboard's own records.

Why learn dead history? Two reasons. The trunk side is still Telnyx — D35 (and its paperwork sibling D51, "caller-ID unverified": the number reads fine but hasn't been authorised in the carrier portal's Outbound Voice Profile) are still the errors you'll see if the trunk-side identity is ever wrong. And the hack is why caller ID lives server-side now — the design only makes sense once you've seen what it replaced.

Today: the switchboard stamps the envelope

The return address is now plain server-side configuration: every Wazo user has an outgoing_caller_id field, and Asterisk stamps it onto every outbound trunk leg. Who fills that field in? Odoo — but remember Lesson 1's three-box rule: enterprise never talks to Wazo. It asks the shop.

🔌 Where this lives in our code

Enterprise computes your caller ID — simply your active DID — and ships it with the user sync (voip/models/voip_extension.py):

def _get_outgoing_caller_id(self):
    did = self.env["voip.did.number"].sudo().search([
        ("extension_id", "=", self.id), ("state", "=", "active"),
    ], order="id desc", limit=1)
    return did.did_number or None
# …passed as sync_user_extension(…, outgoing_caller_id=…) through the shop

The shop is the only one holding a Wazo pen (phone_service_pbx_tenant_ops.py):

def _op_update_user_caller_id(self, *, user_id, outgoing_caller_id):
    self._wazo().users.update(user_id, {"outgoing_caller_id": outgoing_caller_id})

And when you lose your last number, enterprise resets you to outgoing_caller_id="default" (voip_did_number.py) — a lost number must stop being presented as your identity. Read it back to yourself: "set the caller ID" = "update one field on the Wazo user, through the shop." No SIP headers in sight.

One more envelope arrives long after the call: the cost lines. Telnyx bills the trunk leg, the shop's call.cost webhooks attach the price to your voip.call — that's Lesson 13.
