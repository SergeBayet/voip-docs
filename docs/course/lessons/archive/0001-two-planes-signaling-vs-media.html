The two planes: signaling vs media

The one mental model that makes every box in our stack — Kamailio, Asterisk, rtpengine, Telnyx — snap into place.

⏱ ~8 minutes🎯 Mission: discuss & change the stack confidently🧩 Foundational spine

You already know the four players. What you're missing is the lens that explains why the switchboard is split into three different programs instead of one. That lens is the oldest idea in telephony, and once you have it you'll never be confused about "which box does what" again.

The big idea. A phone call is really two separate conversations that travel on different paths:
  • Signaling — the negotiation: "ring this person, here are my codecs, … they answered, … goodbye." Small text messages. This is SIP.
  • Media — the actual voice: a firehose of audio packets. This is RTP (encrypted: SRTP).
Keeping them separate is a deliberate design choice in the SIP standard itself.RFC 3261

Think of an old hotel switchboard. The operator takes your request ("connect me to room 12") and plugs a cable — that's signaling. But your actual voice then travels down the copper wire she plugged — that's media. The operator never hears your conversation; she just sets up and tears down the path. SIP is the operator's clipboard; RTP is the copper.

◆ Signaling plane — SIP

  • "ring / who / what codec / bye"
  • tiny text messages, infrequent
  • routes through proxies
  • fine over TCP/443, like HTTPS

● Media plane — RTP / SRTP

  • the real audio bytes
  • continuous stream, ~50 packets/sec
  • wants the most direct path
  • needs wide UDP range (10000–20000)

Our stack, drawn on the two planes

ComponentPlaneIts one job
KamailiosignalingThe SIP edge: terminate browser WSS, handle registrations at scale, be the hardened front door. Never touches audio.
AsteriskbothThe call engine (B2BUA): dialplan, IVR, voicemail, transfer, queues. Decides the call; delegates the audio.
rtpenginemediaThe audio relay: shuttles and transcodes RTP/SRTP. The only one that touches your voice.
SoftphonebothSends SIP-over-WSS and WebRTC audio. Your handset is on both lanes.
Odoo / IAPconfigNot on the live call at all — they configure the call ahead of time and read reports after (REST + webhooks).

So that's why Kamailio isn't just "another Asterisk"

Your original question — "we already have a PBX, why a second SIP thing?" — dissolves once you see the planes. Asterisk is a brilliant call engine but a mediocre edge; Kamailio is the reverse. They're complementary, and the classic carrier pattern is Kamailio at the edge, Asterisk behind it.nickvsnetworking

  • Scale: Kamailio is stateless-ish and does no media, so it routes thousands of SIP messages/sec and holds 100k+ registrations on modest hardware. Asterisk would choke on that churn.kamailio FAQ
  • Security: the public SIP port is hammered by scanners and toll-fraud bots 24/7. Kamailio is the bouncer — rate-limit, block, hide the internal Asterisk.
  • Division of labour: Kamailio = traffic control (signaling). Asterisk = the call's actual features (media + logic). This is exactly how Wazo packages them.wazo docs
The payoff for our migration. Today the browser registers straight to sip.telnyx.com — Telnyx is our SIP edge. Bringing Wazo in-house means we now run that edge (Kamailio). That's not new work for its own sake — owning the signaling edge is what lets us give each customer their own SIP context (killing the cross-tenant caller-ID spoofing risk) and set caller-ID server-side instead of with the browser P-Asserted-Identity hack.
