Inbound: the world calls you

How a call from any phone on Earth finds your browser and makes it ring — while the call card runs a relay race through the shop to appear in your Odoo.

⏱ ~10 minutes🎯 Goal: trace an inbound call end-to-end, shop hop included🧩 Builds on: Lessons 2–4
Start simple 🧒

A big hotel has one public phone number on its sign, but hundreds of rooms inside. When you call it:

  1. The phone company delivers your call to the hotel's front desk.
  2. The front desk has a little list: "calls to our main number → ring room 12" — or ring the whole third floor at once, or seat the caller in the lobby until someone is free.
  3. And the guest log? The front desk never writes in your diary itself. It rings the hotel chain's head office, which stamps the note and drops it in your mailbox.

The little list — "public number → what rings" — is called the incall. The stamped note travelling desk → head office → you is the call card. Both are new in this lesson.

The journey, as a picture

Zoom in on the PBX (Lesson 2 reminder). "Wazo" is still two programs: Kamailio (the edge) receives the inbound INVITE off the trunk, and Asterisk (the engine) does the incall lookup and rings you. Same split as outbound — just the other door.

New idea #1: the incall (DID → destination)

The whole trick of inbound is one routing rule that answers "a call arrived for this public number — what should ring?"

Nobody answers? Wazo walks the user's fallbacks: another extension, a forward to some outside number, or a voicemail box that plays a custom greeting and takes a message (that whole pipeline is Lesson 12).

New idea #2: the call card's relay race 🏃

Wazo never calls your DB. The event that becomes your call card is a baton passed through three hands — and each runner stamps it:

  • Rename: Wazo speaks its own dialect (call_created, call_ended); the shop translates to the stable names your DB knows (call.initiated, call.hangup). call_ended gains a computed disposition: answered / busy / rejected / missed.
  • Leg filters: a call has several "legs" (one per participant). The shop forwards only the legs you care about — your softphone-recipient leg, or the inbound external leg — so your DB isn't spammed with the rest.
  • Dedupe: event_id = "<call_id>:<event_name>" is UNIQUE in voip.pbx.event — a redelivered event is a harmless no-op.
  • Redelivery, not retry tables: if your DB doesn't ack, the shop answers 503 to webhookd — and Wazo redelivers later. The retry queue lives upstream; nobody builds a new one.
  • Two-clock discipline: occurred_at always carries the PBX clock, never now() — the event tells you when it happened, not when it finally arrived.
Sleeping browsers: call.push / call.push_cancel. A browser tab that's asleep has no SIP connection to ring. Wazo emits push events that travel the same relay and wake the browser via a service-worker push notification — an interim mechanism ("waiting for Kamailio"). Declining from that notification hangs the call up server-side through the shop, because there is no browser SIP session to send the reject.

The payoff: one inbound call lights up all three planes

◆ SignalingThe INVITE over WSS and your 200 OK — the ring and the answer.
● MediaThe actual voice — RTP/SRTP bridged PSTN ↔ trunk ↔ your browser.
⬢ ConfigThe call card — a webhook relay race Wazo → shop → your DB.

The ring and the call card are different lanes. Your browser can ring even if your DB is briefly slow — and the call card, which now crosses one extra building, can appear slightly after the ring. That's by design, not a bug.

Why you must be "registered" (Lesson 3 callback). The incall points at your extension — but Asterisk rings it wherever it last saw a REGISTER from you. No registration (browser closed, not logged in) → there's nowhere to send the INVITE → the call goes straight to your fallbacks or voicemail. Registration is what makes your desk reachable.

Browser niceties (polish, not core): with several tabs open only one plays the ringtone (the "ringleader"), a background tab can ring via a service-worker notification, and Do Not Disturb silences everything.

🔌 Where this lives in our code
  • The incall route — voip.did.number._sync_pbx_incall() asks the shop (sync_incall) to route the DID to the extension's destination — user, group, or queue; the PBX ids come back as pbx_incall_id / pbx_incall_extension_id.
  • Shop-side relay — IAP phone_service: on_wazo_call_event in controllers/pbx_controller.py + renames/leg-filters in utils/wazo_events.py.
  • The doorbell — /voip/api/event (voip/controllers/phone_service_api_controller.py) verifies the HMAC, then voip.pbx.event._handle_call_event() (voip/models/voip_pbx_event.py) dedupes and creates/updates voip.call + bus-notifies your devices.
